נֶשֶׁר‎ noun masculineDeut 32:11 griffon-vulture, eagle (Late Hebrew id.; Assyrian našru; נַשְׁרָא ᵑ7‎; Syriac Arabic vulture (Lane2780), vulgar Ethiopic Di641; Sabean נסר‎ proper name, of deity, and נסר ביתן‎ DHMZMG. xxix (1875), 600; xxxvii (1883), 356); — absolute ׳נ‎ Hosea 8:1 +; נָָֽשֶׁד‎ Deut 28:49 +; plural נְשָׁרִים‎ 2Sam 1:23 +, construct נִשְׁרֵי‎ Lam 4:19; — sometimes (perhaps not always) the griffon-vulture (TristrNHB 172 ff. DrDeut 14:12 NowArchi i. 84 Lane2780), Micah 1:16 (bald, in simile) flying swiftly to pery Hab 1:8; Job 9:26, compare Prov 30:17 (׳בְּנֵינֿ‎, unclean Lev 11:13; Deut 14:12; soaring Job 30:27, also in simile Prov 23:5; Isa 40:31; Obadiah 4, building nest high Jer 49:16 (compare also Job 39:27); as swift also 2Sam 1:23, especially of Babylonian and Assyr. invader Jer 4:13; Lam 4:19, compare Hosea 8:1; Deut 28:49; Jer 48:40; 49:22 (all in comparisons), הַגָּדוֺל הַנֶּשֶׁר‎ Ezek 17:3 (figurativeof Nebuch.), 17:7 (figurative of king of Egypt); leaving no trace of light Prov 30:19; as renewing youth (by moulting) Psa 103:5; as training and supporting its young Exod 19:4 (E), Deut 32:11 (song); נ ׳מְּני‎ of cherubim in vision Ezek 1:10; 10:4. — Only Micah 1:16 seems to compel reference to vulture (on eating fresh carrion by eagles see references in DiLev 11:13), and ׳נ‎ may be a more comprehensive word, including both vulture and eagle.
